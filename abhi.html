Policing and Tracking Using AI

By Abhi Patel

You’re Being Watched: How Government Surveillance Reaches Into Everyday Life

You walk down a city street, pass a few cameras, unlock your phone, and check social media. It feels ordinary — but in those few moments, data about you has already been collected, analyzed, and stored.

The Many Eyes of Modern Surveillance

Government surveillance is no longer something distant or theoretical. It’s part of everyday life, woven quietly into the technologies we rely on. Whether it’s facial recognition in airports, camera networks on street corners, or phone apps that record your location, the line between privacy and safety grows thinner every year.

In the past decade, governments and law enforcement agencies have adopted increasingly powerful technologies to monitor the public. These tools are not evenly distributed — camera coverage varies widely from city to city.

In New Orleans, for example, police secretly used nearly 200 facial-recognition cameras across the city — without telling residents. The Department of Homeland Security has purchased cellphone location data from brokers, sidestepping the need for warrants. Even the U.S. military has partnered with private companies such as Anomaly Six, which harvests location information from ordinary smartphone apps.

Meanwhile, the Transportation Security Administration (TSA) now scans faces at more than 80 airports nationwide, with plans to expand the program to hundreds more. For most travelers, opting out is possible — but few even realize they’re being scanned.

“Every camera, every scan, every data purchase adds up to a vast network that can follow ordinary citizens through both physical and digital spaces.”

The Human Cost of High-Tech Policing

Proponents argue that surveillance makes society safer. But the systems are far from perfect — and the consequences of mistakes can be life-changing. In April 2025, a Brooklyn man named Trevis Williams was wrongfully arrested after police relied on facial recognition software that misidentified him.

Beyond individual errors, these technologies raise deeper questions about fairness. Studies show that facial-recognition tools are less accurate for women, people of color, children, and the elderly. When these systems feed into real-world policing, the result is unequal surveillance — with some communities watched more closely than others.

Once your biometric data — your face, fingerprint, or DNA — enters a database, you can’t easily remove it. The information that defines who you are becomes something owned and controlled by others.

Pushback and Policy Changes

Growing public concern is prompting lawmakers and civil-rights advocates to act. The Traveler Privacy Protection Act of 2025 would require clear consent before airports use facial-recognition systems and limit how long biometric data can be stored. Similarly, the Minnesota Consumer Data Privacy Act gives residents the right to know what data companies collect and demand its deletion.

Across the country, civil-liberties groups are filing lawsuits and demanding transparency from police departments that deploy AI surveillance without oversight. These efforts represent a broader movement to reclaim privacy as a fundamental right — one that keeps pace with technology’s rapid advance.

Taking Back Control of Your Privacy

You don’t need to be an expert to protect yourself. Small choices make a difference:

  • Turn off location tracking for apps that don’t need it.
  • Opt out of facial-recognition features whenever possible.
  • Review privacy settings on social media and devices regularly.
  • Support digital-privacy legislation in your community.
  • Stay informed about how your data is used — awareness is power.

The Balance Ahead

Technology isn’t inherently good or bad — it’s shaped by how we use it. Surveillance can protect people, but unchecked, it erodes the very freedom it claims to safeguard.

Understanding how governments and companies track us isn’t just about avoiding ads or protecting identity; it’s about ensuring accountability and fairness in a world built on data.

“Privacy shouldn’t be a privilege. It’s a shared responsibility — one that depends on all of us knowing who’s watching, and demanding to know why.”
